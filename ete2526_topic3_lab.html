2.1 Orientation

This notebook is the hands-on companion to Sessions 3 and 4 of the Spatial Econometrics module. The theoretical backbone — OLS failure modes in space, the role of the weights matrix W, the SAR/SEM/SLX/SDM/SDEM taxonomy, spatial multipliers, and the LeSage–Pace impact decomposition — was introduced in the lecture. Here you apply those ideas to two real datasets: sub-municipal housing listing zones in Aveiro/Ílhavo, and municipalities across mainland Portugal.

The lab is divided into two tracks. Track A (cells A1–A12) works with point listings aggregated to teaching zones in Aveiro and Ílhavo. The unit of observation after aggregation is the zone, not the individual dwelling, and the key analytical challenge is that the same point listings produce different spatial diagnostics depending on how they are aggregated — a concrete illustration of the Modifiable Areal Unit Problem (MAUP). Track B (cells B1–B10) uses a national municipal analytical layer that already combines boundary polygons with housing-price targets and socio-economic/geographic indicators.

The two tracks ask structurally different spatial questions. In Track A the spatial process concerns micro-scale neighbourhood interaction and local amenity diffusion. In Track B the spatial process concerns macro-scale regional contagion and municipal-level socio-economic sorting. Do not force the two tracks into the same narrative.

2.1.1 Learning Objectives

By the end of this lab, students should be able to:

  • explain why spatial dependence in housing prices invalidates the i.i.d. assumption in standard OLS;
  • distinguish spatial dependence from spatial heterogeneity and from the MAUP;
  • construct and diagnose spatial weights (Queen, Rook, kNN) for polygon layers;
  • use OLS as a transparent benchmark and locate its residuals on a Moran scatter plot;
  • interpret Moran’s I on OLS residuals and explain what a significant positive value implies;
  • apply the Elhorst (2010) decision tree: read the joint result of Robust LM_ρ and Robust LM_λ to identify the first spatial model to estimate, then simplify via LR tests toward SAR, SEM, or SLX;
  • place SLX, SAR/SLM, SEM, SDM, and SDEM on the endogenous/exogenous/error-term interaction taxonomy;
  • explain why β in a SAR or SDM model is not the marginal effect of X on Y;
  • compute and interpret direct, indirect, and total impacts via the LeSage–Pace (I − ρW)⁻¹ framework;
  • state the limits of observational housing data and municipal-level associations.

2.3.1 Methodological Framework

Both tracks in this notebook follow the same six-step analytical pipeline. The steps are identical in structure; what changes is the spatial scale, the level of observation, and the W matrix that is defensible for the process being modelled.

Step Action Output
1. Data audit Load layers; verify CRS, row counts, join keys; flag missingness and outliers. Clean analytical table in EPSG:3763.
2. W construction Build Queen, Rook, and kNN weight matrices; diagnose islands, components, and cardinality distribution. Normalised W objects.
3. Moran’s I (outcome) Test whether the price outcome is spatially autocorrelated before any covariate enters. I statistic, permutation p-value.
4. OLS benchmark Estimate hedonic OLS with HC3 standard errors; retain residuals. Coefficient table, R², AIC.
5. Residual diagnostics Residual Moran’s I; joint Robust LM_ρ and Robust LM_λ tests → Elhorst (2010) three-branch decision. Entry point: SDM (left/right branches) or SLX (middle branch).
6. Spatial models Estimate the Elhorst-indicated model first (SDM or SLX); simplify via LR tests (LR_θ for SAR, LR_λ Common Factor for SEM); compare all specifications. LR test results, impact decomposition, residual Moran, AIC.

The pipeline is deliberately transparent: each step produces a documented output that feeds the next. Robustness checks in A12 and B10 repeat the pipeline under perturbations of W, the dependent variable, and the analytical sample.

2.3.2 Technical Note 1 — OLS with HC3 Standard Errors

Throughout the notebook, the OLS benchmark is estimated with HC3 heteroskedasticity-consistent standard errors (white_se=True in spreg.OLS, or cov_type='HC3' in statsmodels). This section explains what HC3 means and why it is preferred here over plain OLS standard errors.

The HC family

Standard OLS inference assumes homoskedasticity: \(\text{Var}(\varepsilon_i) = \sigma^2\) for all \(i\). When this fails, OLS coefficients remain unbiased but their standard errors are wrong — typically too small — leading to over-rejection of true null hypotheses. The heteroskedasticity-consistent (HC) covariance estimators correct the sandwich formula \(\hat{V} = (X^\top X)^{-1} \hat{\Omega} (X^\top X)^{-1}\) using the observed residuals, without assuming any particular form for the variance function.

Estimator Residual weight \(\hat{\omega}_i\) Notes
HC0 \(\hat{e}_i^2\) White (1980) original. Downward-biased in small samples.
HC1 \(\dfrac{n}{n-k}\,\hat{e}_i^2\) Degrees-of-freedom scaling; small improvement.
HC2 \(\dfrac{\hat{e}_i^2}{1 - h_{ii}}\) Inflates by leverage \(h_{ii}\) (hat-matrix diagonal).
HC3 \(\dfrac{\hat{e}_i^2}{(1 - h_{ii})^2}\) Squares the leverage penalty. Conservative; recommended in small samples.

Here \(h_{ii} = x_i^\top (X^\top X)^{-1} x_i\) is the leverage of observation \(i\) — it measures how far \(x_i\) lies from the centre of the covariate cloud and, consequently, how much the fitted line is pulled toward observation \(i\)’s outcome. A high-leverage observation has \(h_{ii}\) close to 1; its squared residual would be severely deflated by the \((1 - h_{ii})\) denominator in HC2. HC3 squares this denominator, inflating such observations’ contribution to the variance estimate even further.

Why HC3 for zone-level hedonic models

In both tracks the unit of observation is an aggregate (a zone or a municipality), not an individual dwelling. Zone medians computed from 3 listings have far more estimation noise than medians computed from 150 listings. The number of underlying observations is therefore an unobserved variance driver, making heteroskedasticity essentially guaranteed. HC3 is the standard choice when:

  • the sample is small (< 250 units), as in Track A (sub-municipal zones);
  • some observations have high leverage (outlier zones with extreme covariate profiles);
  • no parametric form for the variance function is available.

Note that HC3 corrects the standard errors of the OLS coefficients; it does not change the coefficient estimates themselves, nor does it address spatial autocorrelation in the residuals — that requires the spatial models in steps A10–A12 and B8–B10.

2.3.3 Technical Note 2 — kNN Weights and Variable Neighbour Counts

When the connection graph for kNN weights is displayed on a map, some zones appear to have many connections and others few, even though kNN is defined as “each zone has exactly \(k\) neighbours.” This is not a bug. It reflects a fundamental structural property of kNN: asymmetry.

Why kNN is asymmetric

The kNN weight matrix is constructed by, for each zone \(i\), finding the \(k\) zones whose centroids are closest to \(i\)’s centroid. Formally, if \(d_{ij}\) is the distance between centroids \(i\) and \(j\):

\[w_{ij} = 1 \text{ if } j \in \text{kNN}(i), \quad w_{ij} = 0 \text{ otherwise}\]

There is no guarantee that \(w_{ij} = w_{ji}\). Zone \(i\) may select zone \(j\) as one of its \(k\) nearest, but zone \(j\) may not select zone \(i\) back — because \(j\) has \(k\) other zones that are even closer to it.

Why the visualised graph shows variable degree

Map visualisations of spatial weight graphs are typically undirected: an edge is drawn between \(i\) and \(j\) if \(w_{ij} > 0\) or \(w_{ji} > 0\). The visible degree of zone \(i\) in this undirected graph is:

\[\deg(i) = \underbrace{k}_{\text{out-degree (fixed)}} + \underbrace{k_{\text{in}}(i)}_{\text{in-degree (varies)}}\]

The in-degree \(k_{\text{in}}(i)\) — how many other zones chose \(i\) as one of their \(k\) nearest — varies across the map:

  • Dense, central zones are geometrically close to many others; many zones pick them → high in-degree → many visible connections.
  • Peripheral or isolated zones are chosen by few others → low in-degree → fewer visible connections.

This is correct and expected. The out-degree is fixed at \(k\) for every zone; only the in-degree varies.

Practical implication for spatial models

The asymmetry of kNN means that the raw weight matrix is not symmetric (\(W \neq W^\top\)). Most spatial model estimators in spreg (and in PySAL generally) require a row-standardised symmetric W. libpysal handles row standardisation automatically (transform='R'), but symmetrisation — if needed — must be requested explicitly. In this notebook, kNN weights are row-standardised but not symmetrised, so the Moran statistic and LM tests use the row-standardised directed form. Moran’s I is defined for asymmetric W and remains valid; the interpretation is unchanged.

Contrast with contiguity weights

Queen and Rook contiguity weights are always symmetric by construction: if zone \(i\) shares a boundary with zone \(j\), then \(j\) shares the same boundary with \(i\). Every zone therefore appears in the connection graph with a degree that equals exactly its number of shared-boundary neighbours — which can still vary (a corner zone has fewer boundary-sharing neighbours than a central zone), but the variability comes from polygon geometry, not from directionality.

2.3.4 Technical Note 3 — The Elhorst (2010) Model-Selection Decision Tree

Model selection in this notebook follows the decision tree of Elhorst (2010) rather than the older Anselin et al. (1996) sequential LM procedure. This note explains the tree, its logic, and why it supersedes the earlier approach.

Why not Anselin (1996)?

The Anselin (1996) procedure uses a sequential rule: if Robust LM-Lag fires and Robust LM-Error does not → estimate SAR; if the reverse → estimate SEM; if both fire → ambiguous, use theory. This has two well-documented shortcomings:

  1. It skips SDM. SAR and SEM are both restricted special cases of the Spatial Durbin Model. Jumping directly to SAR or SEM imposes restrictions (θ = 0 for SAR; θ = −ρβ for SEM) that are not tested — they are assumed by the selection rule.
  2. The “both significant” case is under-specified. When both robust LM tests reject simultaneously — which is common in large samples — the Anselin rule offers no principled starting point.

The Elhorst (2010) tree

The key insight is that SDM is the general nesting model: it contains SAR (if θ = 0) and is related to SEM via the Common Factor Hypothesis (θ = −ρβ). Rather than jumping to a restricted model, Elhorst proposes starting at SDM and simplifying downward via Likelihood Ratio (LR) tests.

Step 1. Estimate OLS. Run both Robust LM_ρ (H₀: ρ = 0) and Robust LM_λ (H₀: λ = 0) simultaneously.

Step 2. Read the joint result and enter the corresponding branch:

Robust LM_ρ Robust LM_λ Branch First model
Reject (ρ ≠ 0) Fail (λ = 0) Left SDM
Fail (ρ = 0) Fail (λ = 0) Middle SLX
Fail (ρ = 0) Reject (λ ≠ 0) Right SDM
Both reject Both reject General SDM (default)

Step 3. Simplify using LR tests:

  • Left branch — SDM → LR_θ (H₀: θ = 0): fail → SAR; reject → keep SDM.
  • Middle branch — SLX → test θ = 0: fail → OLS; reject → keep SLX or run LR_ρ to consider SDM.
  • Right branch — SDM → LR_λ (Common Factor H₀: θ + ρβ = 0): fail → SEM; reject → keep SDM.

  • Theory still governs. The tree is a formal starting-point rule, not an automatic selector. A strong theoretical prior (e.g., demand-displacement contagion → SAR) should inform interpretation alongside LR test results.
  • All five models are still estimated (SLX, SAR, SEM, SDM, SDEM) for comparison. The Elhorst tree identifies the primary specification and which restrictions to formally test; it does not preclude estimating alternatives.

2.3.5 Technical Note 4 — Maximum Likelihood Estimation vs. OLS: Two Philosophies of “Finding the Best Answer”

This notebook uses Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE) for all spatial models (SAR, SEM, SDM, SDEM). OLS is reserved for the benchmark and for the residual diagnostic step. Understanding why spatial models require MLE — and what makes it fundamentally different from OLS — is essential for interpreting the output correctly.

OLS: an algebraic solution

OLS finds the coefficient vector \(\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}\) that minimises the sum of squared residuals:

\[\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}_{\text{OLS}} = \arg\min_{\boldsymbol{\beta}} \sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - x_i^\top \boldsymbol{\beta})^2 = (X^\top X)^{-1} X^\top y\]

The key word is algebraic: given any dataset, you substitute the numbers and turn the handle. No distributional assumption is needed to compute the point estimates (though normality is assumed for inference). The formula is exact and closed-form — there is no search, no iteration, no probability model embedded in the estimation step itself.

Analogy — the tailor. OLS is like a tailor who fits a suit by minimising the total length of all gaps between fabric edges. The criterion is purely geometric: make the misfit as small as possible. The tailor does not ask “what is the probability distribution of body shapes?”; the criterion is purely mechanical.

MLE: a probabilistic search

MLE starts from a different question. Instead of “what parameters make the residuals small?”, it asks: “what parameter values make the data we actually observed as probable as possible?”

Formally, assume the outcome follows a probability distribution with density \(f(y \mid \boldsymbol{\theta})\), where \(\boldsymbol{\theta}\) collects all unknown parameters. For \(n\) independent observations the joint density is:

\[L(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \prod_{i=1}^n f(y_i \mid \boldsymbol{\theta})\]

This is called the likelihood function — it is the joint density of the data, viewed as a function of the parameters rather than of the data. MLE finds:

\[\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_{\text{MLE}} = \arg\max_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} \ln L(\boldsymbol{\theta})\]

(The log-likelihood \(\ln L\) is maximised instead of \(L\) itself for computational convenience; both give the same solution.) Unlike the OLS formula, the MLE solution generally has no closed form and is found via iterative numerical algorithms (e.g., Newton–Raphson, BFGS).

Analogy — the detective. MLE is like a detective who, having found a particular set of clues (the observed data), asks: “under which version of events (parameter values) were these clues most likely to have appeared?” The detective does not minimise the distance between clues and any line; they reason about probabilities and choose the story that best explains what they found.

Why OLS fails for spatial lag models — and MLE rescues it

In the standard linear model, OLS is unbiased because \(X\) is exogenous: \(\mathbb{E}[\varepsilon \mid X] = 0\). The spatial lag model (SAR) adds \(\rho W y\) as a regressor:

\[y = \rho W y + X\boldsymbol{\beta} + \varepsilon\]

The term \(Wy\) is an endogenous regressor: it is literally a weighted average of \(y\) values, which share the same error \(\varepsilon\). OLS treats \(Wy\) as if it were exogenous and produces biased and inconsistent estimates of \(\rho\) — the bias does not vanish even in large samples.

MLE handles this correctly. The spatial lag model can be written in reduced form as \(y = (I - \rho W)^{-1}(X\boldsymbol{\beta} + \varepsilon)\), and the likelihood is derived from the full joint distribution of \(y\), including the Jacobian term \(\ln|I - \rho W|\) that accounts for the spatial feedback loop. Maximising this likelihood yields consistent, asymptotically efficient estimates.

OLS vs. MLE: a summary for social scientists

Key takeaway. In a world with no spatial dependence, OLS and MLE (under normality) produce identical coefficient estimates. The moment you add a spatial structure to the model, MLE is the necessary tool — not because it is theoretically superior in the abstract, but because the spatial feedback invalidates the exogeneity assumption that OLS requires.

2.3.6 Technical Note 5 — Lagrange Multipliers: Constrained Optimisation and its Role in Hypothesis Testing

The Lagrange Multiplier (LM) tests used in the residual diagnostic step (Step 5 of the pipeline) take their name directly from the mathematical technique of Lagrange multipliers. Understanding the technique illuminates why the tests work the way they do — and why they can be computed from OLS residuals alone, without ever estimating the spatial model.

The mathematical idea: optimising under a constraint

Suppose you want to maximise a function \(f(x, y)\) subject to the constraint \(g(x, y) = 0\). The Lagrangian is:

\[\mathcal{L}(x, y, \lambda) = f(x, y) - \lambda \cdot g(x, y)\]

Setting \(\nabla_{x,y} \mathcal{L} = 0\) and \(g(x,y) = 0\) jointly characterises the constrained optimum. The scalar \(\lambda\) — the Lagrange multiplier — emerges as a by-product of the solution and has a precise interpretation:

\[\lambda = \frac{\partial f^*}{\partial c} \quad \text{where the constraint is } g(x,y) = c\]

That is, \(\lambda\) measures how much the optimal value of the objective would increase if the constraint were relaxed by one unit. A large \(|\lambda|\) means the constraint is severely binding — the constrained optimum is far from the unconstrained one. \(\lambda \approx 0\) means the constraint is nearly non-binding — relaxing it would change almost nothing.

Analogy — the budget constraint. A student maximising the number of chapters read faces a hard constraint of 24 hours per day. The Lagrange multiplier on that constraint answers: “if I somehow gained one extra hour, how many more chapters could I read?” If the answer is large, the constraint is very tight. If the answer is near zero, the student already has plenty of slack and the time limit does not bind.

The statistical translation: the score test

In a statistical context, maximising the log-likelihood is itself an optimisation problem. Imposing a null hypothesis — e.g., \(H_0: \rho = 0\) — is imposing a constraint on the parameter space. The Lagrangian becomes:

\[\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}, \lambda) = \ln L(\boldsymbol{\theta}) - \lambda \cdot h(\boldsymbol{\theta})\]

where \(h(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = 0\) encodes the null. At the constrained maximum (the restricted estimate \(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_0\)), the gradient of the log-likelihood — called the score — is not generally zero:

\[s(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_0) = \left.\frac{\partial \ln L}{\partial \boldsymbol{\theta}}\right|_{\boldsymbol{\theta} = \hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_0} \neq \mathbf{0}\]

The score is being “held back” by the constraint, exactly as a hiker on a fixed trail is held back from the true summit. The LM test statistic measures whether this score is significantly different from zero:

\[\text{LM} = s(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_0)^\top \, \mathcal{I}(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_0)^{-1} \, s(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_0) \;\xrightarrow{d}\; \chi^2_q\]

where \(\mathcal{I}(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_0)\) is the Fisher information matrix — the expected curvature of the log-likelihood — evaluated at the restricted estimate, and \(q\) is the number of imposed restrictions.

The practical payoff: only the restricted model is needed

The decisive practical advantage of the LM test is that the score \(s(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_0)\) is evaluated at the restricted estimate. In the pipeline of this notebook:

  • \(H_0: \rho = 0\) (no spatial lag) → the restricted model is OLS.
  • \(H_0: \lambda = 0\) (no spatial error) → the restricted model is OLS.

Both tests are computed directly from OLS residuals and the spatial weight matrix \(W\), without ever estimating SAR or SEM. This makes them natural screening tools: run OLS, inspect the LM diagnostics, and only estimate the more expensive spatial model if the diagnostics suggest it is needed.

Key intuition. The LM statistic asks: “If we were allowed to relax the restriction \(\rho = 0\), how much would the log-likelihood improve at the margin?” A large value means the spatial model is substantially better — the null is binding and should be rejected. A small value means OLS is already near the unconstrained maximum — spatial dependence is not detectable in this sample.

Robust vs. standard LM tests

The standard LM_ρ and LM_λ tests assume the alternative is only a spatial lag or only a spatial error. When both forms of dependence are present, each standard test is contaminated by the other, inflating its size. The Robust versions (Robust LM_ρ, Robust LM_λ) adjust the score for the presence of the other alternative, making them approximately independent and jointly usable — which is exactly what the Elhorst (2010) decision tree requires (see Technical Note 3).

2.3.7 Technical Note 6 — The Likelihood Ratio: Comparing Models by How Well They Explain the Data

Once the spatial models have been estimated (Steps A10–A12, B8–B10), the notebook uses Likelihood Ratio (LR) tests to decide whether a simpler, restricted model is statistically indistinguishable from the more general one. This note explains what a likelihood ratio is, why it is the natural tool for comparing nested models, and how to read the test output.

The likelihood ratio defined

Let \(M_1\) be an unrestricted model with \(p\) free parameters and log-likelihood \(\ln \hat{L}_1\), and let \(M_0\) be a restricted model nested within \(M_1\) — it imposes \(q\) constraints, leaving \(p - q\) free parameters and log-likelihood \(\ln \hat{L}_0\). Because \(M_1\) has more freedom, it will always fit at least as well: \(\ln \hat{L}_1 \geq \ln \hat{L}_0\).

The likelihood ratio statistic measures how much better \(M_1\) fits:

\[\text{LR} = -2 \left( \ln \hat{L}_0 - \ln \hat{L}_1 \right) = 2 \left( \ln \hat{L}_1 - \ln \hat{L}_0 \right) \geq 0\]

Under \(H_0\) (the restrictions are true), \(\text{LR} \xrightarrow{d} \chi^2_q\) by Wilks’ theorem. A large LR means the restrictions cost a lot of fit — reject \(H_0\), keep the general model. A small LR means the restrictions are essentially free — fail to reject, prefer the simpler model.

Analogy — two cameras. Imagine two photographers, one with a basic camera (\(M_0\), fewer parameters) and one with a professional camera (\(M_1\), more parameters), both photographing the same scene (the data). The LR test asks: “Is the professional camera’s photo significantly sharper, or is the improvement just noise?” If the improvement is negligible, the basic camera is good enough — simplify. If the difference is large, the professional camera captures something real — keep the general model.

Why “−2 times” the log-ratio?

The factor of \(-2\) is not arbitrary. By Wilks’ theorem (1938):

\[-2 \ln \frac{L(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_0)}{L(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_1)} = -2\bigl(\ln \hat{L}_0 - \ln \hat{L}_1\bigr) \;\xrightarrow{d}\; \chi^2_q\]

The \(-2\) rescaling transforms the log-ratio into a statistic with a known limiting distribution, making critical values and p-values available from standard tables. Intuitively, the \(\chi^2_q\) distribution arises because each restriction contributes one “squared standard normal” unit of information to the test.

Three tests, one null hypothesis

There are three classical ways to test the same null hypothesis \(H_0: h(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = 0\). They converge asymptotically but differ in what they require to compute:

Test What you estimate Core question
Wald (W) Unrestricted model only Is \(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_1\) far from satisfying \(h(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = 0\)?
LM (Score) Restricted model only Is the score at \(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_0\) significantly non-zero?
LR Both models Is \(\ln \hat{L}_1 - \ln \hat{L}_0\) significantly large?

In this notebook: the LM tests in Step 5 use only the OLS (restricted) model. The LR tests in Step 6 compare two already estimated spatial models — so both likelihoods are available and the LR is the natural choice.

Reading LR output in the notebook

Each LR test table reports the LR statistic, degrees of freedom df (= number of constraints), and p-value. The restrictions tested and their interpretations are:

Restriction tested \(H_0\) Unrestricted → Restricted If rejected:
LR_θ (SAR restriction) \(\boldsymbol{\theta} = \mathbf{0}\) SDM → SAR Spatial lag of \(X\) matters; keep SDM
LR_λ (Common Factor) \(\boldsymbol{\theta} + \rho \boldsymbol{\beta} = \mathbf{0}\) SDM → SEM Common Factor assumption fails; keep SDM
LR_ρ (no lag) \(\rho = 0\) SDM → SLX Spatial feedback present; SDM preferred over SLX

Key takeaway. The LR statistic is a direct measure of the cost of simplification in units of log-likelihood. Because log-likelihood measures how well the model explains the data, the LR test provides a principled, model-based answer to the question: “Can we afford to use the simpler model?” If the cost is small relative to \(\chi^2_q\) critical values, we can. If it is large, the data are signalling that the restriction matters and the general model should be retained.

2.4.1 A1. Conceptual Foundation and Data Loading

Why housing prices are spatial

The baseline OLS model assumes y = Xβ + ε with Var(ε) = σ²I — errors are independent and identically distributed. For housing prices, this assumption is implausible for at least three distinct reasons that map directly onto the spatial model taxonomy taught in the lecture:

Endogenous spatial interaction (SAR mechanism): the asking price in zone i is influenced by asking prices in neighbouring zones j, because buyers compare options across zones simultaneously and agents adjust valuations in response to what is happening nearby. This mutual dependence means Cov(Wy, ε) ≠ 0, making the spatial lag of the outcome Wy endogenous. OLS applied to y = ρWy + Xβ + ε is biased and inconsistent. Maximum likelihood estimation via spreg.ML_Lag is required.

Spatially structured unobservables (SEM mechanism): omitted neighbourhood characteristics — proximity to the Ria de Aveiro, local planning constraints, social composition, microclimate — are spatially correlated. These factors end up in the error term, producing a structured error process u = λWu + ε. OLS on y = Xβ + u remains unbiased but is inefficient, and standard errors are downward-biased, leading to over-rejection of null hypotheses. Estimation via spreg.ML_Error corrects the standard errors and restores efficiency.

Exogenous spatial interaction (SLX mechanism): the amenity or covariate context of neighbouring zones affects prices in zone i without requiring price contagion. For example, high essential_poi_density in a neighbouring zone may raise prices in the focal zone through spillover demand. The spatially lagged covariate WX is exogenous, so OLS on y = Xβ + WXθ + ε remains valid and consistent. The θ coefficient captures these first-order, local spillovers.

The conceptual distinction between these three mechanisms — summarised in the Elhorst interaction-effects taxonomy — determines which model is appropriate. The data cannot resolve this on their own; theory about the housing-market process must guide specification.

Interpreting the listing QA diagnostics

Missingness: missing prices or areas are not zero values — they are unobserved. Zone medians will be computed from observed listings only. High missingness for a key variable (>20% of price_eur) should trigger a decision about whether to use that variable as the primary outcome or demote it to a robustness check.

Coordinate quality and zone_match_method: listings assigned by nearest-zone proximity are not automatically wrong, but they inflate the apparent supply in peripheral zones and can create spatial autocorrelation artefacts that mimic genuine neighbourhood price spillovers. The fallback_assignment_share column in master_zones will flag zones where this matters.

Condition score decision: infer_condition_score maps preservation/condition labels to an ordinal score only when the observed values form a defensible rank order. If the decision shows condition_score_created: False, the model will use category share columns as proxies instead.

2.4.3 A3. Zones, Parishes, and Analytical Supports

The same listing points can be aggregated to zones, parishes, square grids, or census tracts. The spatial pattern of prices and the Moran statistic will differ across these choices. This is the Modifiable Areal Unit Problem (MAUP): the apparent spatial structure of the housing market depends partly on the analytical support chosen, not just on the underlying market process.

What the code does: - Joins listing-level data to zone polygons via zone_id, computing zone-level medians, means, IQRs, category shares, fallback-assignment rates, and outlier shares via aggregate_listing_support. - Creates master_zones as the primary modelling table (all 131 zones, including those with no listings). - If the parish layer is available, creates master_parishes as a parallel sensitivity support. - Reports a support summary: how many zones have at least one listing, fewer than 3, 5, or 10.

Interpreting the zone support summary

Zones with zero listings are a normal feature of a well-specified modelling table, not an error. They arise when a zone covers an area where no dwellings were listed during 2018–2022. Zero-listing zones should not be dropped silently: in spatial modelling they affect contiguity relationships and Moran statistics. A zone that is a spatial neighbour of high-price zones but has no observations is a missing value in the Moran calculation — a materially different signal from a low-price zone.

Small-N flags (small_n_lt3, small_n_lt5, small_n_lt10) record zones where the median price estimate is based on very few listings. A zone-level median from 2 listings has a wide implicit confidence interval and is strongly influenced by the composition of those 2 listings. The robustness matrix (section A12) will re-run models after filtering to zones with ≥3 and ≥5 listings.

Fallback assignment share: zones with >25% nearest-zone fallback assignments should be interpreted cautiously; their price aggregates may include listings more accurately attributed to adjacent zones.

Interpreting the exploratory maps

Median total price vs. median unit price: these two maps tell different stories. A zone with high median total price but moderate unit price most likely contains larger dwellings (houses rather than apartments). A zone with high unit price but moderate total price typically contains smaller, higher-quality, or more centrally located units. Interpreting total price as a quality measure is misleading when dwelling sizes vary systematically across zones.

Spatial clustering: if the maps show visible clusters of high-price and low-price zones — contiguous blocks of similar colours — this is the first visual evidence of positive spatial autocorrelation. Moran’s I (section A7) will quantify and test this pattern. Recall that Moran’s I and W were introduced in the ESDA sessions (Sessions 1–2); here they bridge from description to model choice.

Size map: comparing the size map with the unit-price map helps distinguish genuinely high-value locations from artefacts of composition. Zones where high median total price co-occurs with high median area and low median unit price are likely house-dominated zones, not necessarily more expensive per unit of housing service.

Interpreting the high-price diagnostic table

Several mechanisms can produce an apparently expensive zone without reflecting genuine locational quality:

  1. Property-type composition: a zone containing predominantly detached houses commands a higher unit price than one with mostly apartments.
  2. Preservation-class composition: a high share of new listings inflates the zone median if new developments are systematically more expensive.
  3. Small-N instability: a zone with 3–5 listings has a median that shifts substantially if any single listing is removed. A wide IQR for a small-N zone is a reliability warning.
  4. Fallback assignments: a zone assigned by nearest-zone proximity may borrow the price signal of an adjacent high-price zone.
  5. Coastal or Ria proximity: zones adjacent to the Ria de Aveiro or with water views tend to command genuine premium prices not explained by observable characteristics — these would appear as positive OLS residuals and spatially structured errors (the SEM mechanism from the lecture).

The table should not be used to drop zones silently. Flag them in the robustness matrix (section A12) instead.

Interpreting the spatial weights diagnostics

Queen vs. Rook: Queen contiguity defines neighbours as zones sharing either an edge or a corner; Rook requires a shared edge. In clean polygon datasets they often agree; where they differ materially, ask whether corner-only proximity is theoretically meaningful for the housing-market process being modelled.

kNN weights: k-nearest-neighbour weights ensure every zone has exactly k neighbours, avoiding the island problem. The trade-off is that kNN imposes a fixed neighbourhood size regardless of polygon size variation and may connect zones across physical barriers — notably, the Ria de Aveiro creates a meaningful barrier that contiguity weights respect implicitly but kNN weights may not.

Islands and components — what this run found: the output reports 5 islands (zone IDs 24, 31, 80, 83, 96) and 9 disconnected components for both Queen and Rook weights. Islands arise where a zone polygon shares no boundary with any other zone in the layer (isolated polygons, topology gaps, or sliver polygons). An island is excluded from Moran’s I and spatial regression because its W row is zero, reducing the effective estimation sample. kNN (k=4 and k=6) connects all 104 zones into one component and resolves the island problem, at the cost of possibly linking some zones across the Ria barrier. One connected component under Queen or Rook would be the ideal for a clean zone layer; the observed fragmentation is a concrete data-quality finding that must be kept in mind when interpreting Moran statistics and model estimates, and motivates including kNN matrices in the robustness checks (section A12).

Why W must be diagnosed before Moran’s I: Moran’s I is the spatial autocovariance of the series weighted by W. A W matrix with many islands, disconnected components, or asymmetric cardinalities produces a Moran statistic that reflects the topology of the weights object rather than the structure of the housing market.

HC3 standard errors: appropriate because zone medians computed from different numbers of listings have unequal precision. The HC3 correction uses leverage-adjusted residuals and performs well in small samples.

Compare to the raw outcome Moran (Queen I=0.277, p_sim=0.002): OLS reduced Moran’s I by roughly 53% under Queen. Only the Rook specification reaches marginal 5% significance (p_sim=0.047). Queen, kNN-4, and kNN-6 residual Moran values are not significant. This pattern suggests that much of the spatial structure in prices is absorbed by the observed covariates, and the remaining residual autocorrelation is weak and W-dependent.

A significant residual Moran’s I does not identify the mechanism. It could arise from omitted spatially correlated covariates, endogenous price interaction (SAR), spatially autocorrelated unobservables (SEM), or spatial heterogeneity. The joint LM diagnostics are the next tool.

Elhorst (2010) decision tree — reading the joint Robust LM result:

Read Robust LM_ρ (H₀: ρ = 0) and Robust LM_λ (H₀: λ = 0) simultaneously, then enter the corresponding branch:

Robust LM_ρ Robust LM_λ Branch First model to estimate LR simplification test
Reject Fail Left SDM LR_θ (H₀: θ = 0): fail → SAR; reject → keep SDM
Fail Fail Middle SLX Test θ = 0: fail → OLS; reject → LR_ρ → SLX or SDM
Fail Reject Right SDM LR_λ Common Factor (H₀: θ + ρβ = 0): fail → SEM; reject → keep SDM
Reject Reject General SDM (default) LR_θ and LR_λ + AIC + theory

The key departure from Anselin et al. (1996): SDM is always the first spatial model when either or both robust LM tests fire. SAR and SEM are not direct selection endpoints — they are simplifications of SDM, confirmed by LR restriction tests. SLX is the natural starting model only when neither test rejects.

Applying the tree to this dataset (Aveiro/Ílhavo zones, N=65) — what this run shows:

Diagnostic Statistic p-value
LM-Lag 2.42 0.120
Robust LM-Lag 2.21 0.137
LM-Error 1.62 0.202
Robust LM-Error 1.42 0.234

Neither Robust LM test rejects (middle branch). This is the outcome the code’s diagnostic table also reports (“No strong SAR/SEM signal; consider OLS/SLX”). The Elhorst (2010) middle branch prescribes SLX as the first spatial model: estimate SLX, then test whether the WX terms (θ) are jointly significant (test θ = 0). If they are not, revert to OLS. If they are, compare SLX to SDM.

The fact that section A10 also estimates SAR, SEM, SDM, and SDEM provides a fuller picture for comparison and robustness, but the formal model-selection entry point given these LM results is SLX, not SDM. All estimates should be interpreted with caution: with N=65, LM tests have moderate power, and the borderline Rook residual Moran (p_sim=0.047) leaves residual spatial dependence as a plausible but not established concern.

Crucial reminder: the decision tree is a formal guide, not an automatic selector. Theory governs final interpretation. Even when the LM tests do not reject, if market theory predicts that zone prices are influenced by adjacent zone prices, a spatial model can be estimated and its fit compared to OLS on theoretical grounds.

2.4.10 A10. Local SLX, SAR/SLM, SEM, SDM, and SDEM

Each model tells a different spatial story and has different implications for interpretation and policy.

What the code does: - Estimates SLX via HC3 OLS after appending WX columns (spatially lagged covariates). - Estimates SAR/SLM and SEM via maximum likelihood using spreg.ML_Lag and spreg.ML_Error. - Estimates SDM — the Elhorst general model — by running ML_Lag on the expanded frame [X, WX_selected]. - Estimates SDEM by running ML_Error on the same expanded frame. - Computes residual Moran’s I for each model to track absorption of spatial autocorrelation. - Assembles a comparison table with fit statistics (pseudo-R², log-likelihood, AIC), spatial parameters (ρ for SAR/SDM; λ for SEM/SDEM), and residual Moran across specifications. - The LR_θ simplification (SDM → SAR) and the Common Factor LR_λ (SDM → SEM) are evaluated from the log-likelihood ratio and ΔAIC in the comparison table.

SLX adds WX (spatially lagged covariates) to OLS. The θ coefficient captures the average effect on zone i’s price of a one-unit change in the covariate value in zone i’s neighbours. SLX produces local spillovers only: a change in zone j affects zone i via the direct WX channel but does not propagate further. SLX is the Elhorst middle-branch model. Given the LM results in section A9, SLX is the principled starting model.

SAR/SLM adds Wy as an endogenous regressor. The coefficient ρ > 0 means a price shock in any zone propagates globally through the network via the spatial multiplier (I − ρW)⁻¹. Critical: β in SAR is not the marginal effect of X on Y — compute direct, indirect, and total impacts (section A11). In this run, ρ = 0.014 is negligible: the global feedback loop is present in theory but empirically trivial.

In the Elhorst tree, SAR emerges from SDM via the LR_θ test (H₀: θ = 0):

\[\text{LR}_\theta = 2(\ell_{\text{SDM}} - \ell_{\text{SAR}}) \sim \chi^2(q)\]

With ℓ_SDM = 8.849, ℓ_SAR = 3.851, and q = 2 WX terms: LR_θ ≈ 10.0 (p ≈ 0.007). The LR_θ test rejects SAR: the WX terms in SDM are jointly significant and SDM is preferred over SAR on likelihood grounds.

SEM models the error process u = λWu + ε. A positive λ means unobserved spatial factors are correlated across adjacent zones. In SEM, β retains a direct interpretation analogous to OLS. However, as noted above, SEM’s residual Moran (0.185) exceeds OLS (0.131) in this run — the SEM error structure does not absorb the spatial autocovariance present in the residuals. This is an important diagnostic failure that limits the credibility of the SEM specification here.

SEM emerges from SDM via the Common Factor LR_λ test (H₀: θ + ρβ = 0):

\[\text{LR}_\lambda = 2(\ell_{\text{SDM}} - \ell_{\text{SEM}}) \sim \chi^2(q)\]

With ℓ_SDM = 8.849, ℓ_SEM = 3.605: LR_λ ≈ 10.5 (p ≈ 0.005). The Common Factor test rejects SEM: SDM and SEM are not statistically equivalent, and the WX channel in SDM carries information beyond what SEM’s error structure captures.

SDM is the Elhorst general model (left and right branches; default for “both reject”). Retain SDM when both LR_θ (SDM vs. SAR) and LR_λ (SDM vs. SEM via Common Factor) reject simultaneously — which is the case here. Despite the middle-branch LM result, the LR restriction tests favour SDM over both SAR and SEM once the models are estimated.

SDEM combines SLX-style WX with SEM-style error correlation. It achieves the best AIC (−2.18) and a moderate residual Moran (0.113). SDEM is a competitive robustness benchmark here: if SDEM and SDM yield similar substantive conclusions, the spatial structure is not sensitive to whether spatial dependence is modelled as endogenous lag (ρ) or as correlated errors (λ) combined with neighbourhood covariate spillovers.

Reading the SAR/SLM impact table — Track A context

In this run, ρ_SAR = 0.014 — essentially zero. When ρ is this small, the spatial multiplier (I − ρW)⁻¹ is nearly identity, so the LeSage–Pace impact decomposition is dominated by the ML-estimated β coefficients, not by feedback propagation.

The comparison table (OLS β vs. SAR direct) reports multiplier_inflation_% values that are negative or near-zero for most predictors (−3.3% for log area, −11.2% for log_n_listings). This does not mean the feedback loop works in reverse; it means the ML joint estimation of ρ and β in SAR yields β estimates that differ from OLS β for reasons unrelated to the multiplier (different estimator, slightly different sample after excluding islands from the W-restricted estimation). With ρ ≈ 0.014, there is no material amplification of own-zone effects through spatial feedback. The sign reversals for property_type_std_share_house (OLS: −0.017 → SAR direct: +0.015) and municipality_Ilhavo (OLS: +0.005 → SAR direct: −0.014) also reflect β instability under joint ML estimation rather than a genuine spatial multiplier effect.

Practical implication: for Track A, the SAR impact decomposition provides limited additional insight beyond OLS because ρ is negligible. The SDM and SDEM models, which achieve better fit (lower AIC, lower residual Moran), are more informative.

Reading the SDM impact table — Track A context

SDM has ρ_SDM = 0.072 and two significant WX terms (W_mean_condition_score with θ = +0.216, p = 0.036; W_mean_log_area with θ = −0.269, p = 0.130 marginal).

  • mean_condition_score: direct impact = 0.157, indirect = 0.189, total = 0.346. The positive indirect effect reflects both ρ propagation and the positive θ: being surrounded by zones with higher condition scores raises own-zone prices. This is the strongest spillover finding in Track A.
  • mean_log_area: direct = 0.453, indirect = −0.196, total = 0.257. The negative indirect effect (θ = −0.269) means zones surrounded by large-dwelling zones have lower prices — consistent with an inverse relationship between neighbourhood size composition and price intensity (larger units in adjacent zones may signal less central or less service-rich locations).

For SDM variables without a WX term (θ = 0), the indirect/direct ratio equals ρ = 0.072, producing small but non-zero indirect effects that are simply the SAR propagation through the multiplier.

Reading the SEM and SDEM tables

In SEM, every row shows direct_impact = β and indirect_impact = 0. The spatial process λWu absorbs clustered unobservables but does not create a mechanism by which a change in zone j’s observable X raises zone i’s price. However, as noted in section A10, SEM’s residual Moran is higher than OLS in this run — the λ process does not fully represent the spatial dependence in the data.

In SDEM, the WX terms (θ) capture first-order, local spillovers from neighbouring zones’ observable covariates. W_mean_condition_score (θ = +0.264, significant) and W_mean_log_area (θ = −0.120, significant) enter y directly but do not propagate through a multiplier.

Practical guidance for scale interpretation

If the dependent variable is log price, all impacts are approximately proportional: - A direct impact of 0.05 ≈ 5 % higher own-zone price per one-unit increase in the predictor. - An indirect impact of 0.03 ≈ 3 % higher prices in neighbouring zones on average.

For share predictors (e.g., condition shares ranging 0–1): - Multiply impacts by 0.10 to get the effect of a 10-percentage-point change.

For log predictors (e.g., log area): - Multiply impacts by ln(1.1) ≈ 0.095 to get the effect of a 10 % increase.

Interpreting the robustness matrix

Stable residual Moran across W specs: if all four W matrices produce a significant positive residual Moran after OLS, the spatial structure in prices is not specific to any one neighbourhood definition. This strengthens the case for a spatial model.

Sensitivity to minimum-listing-count thresholds: if results change substantially when zones with <3 or <5 listings are excluded, small-N instability is influencing the aggregate pattern.

Sensitivity to outlier and fallback filters: large changes in R² or residual Moran when high-outlier-share or high-fallback-share zones are removed indicate those zones disproportionately drive the spatial pattern. Investigate those zones before drawing substantive conclusions.

2.5 Track B — Mainland Portugal Municipal Analysis

Track B shifts to the national scale. The unit of observation is the municipality (278 mainland municipalities). Dependent variables are municipal housing-price statistics: median sales prices (total, new, existing stock) and median rent. Explanatory variables are municipal indicators covering demographic pressure, human capital, housing stock, land use, accessibility, and tourism.

The spatial process at municipal scale differs from the sub-municipal process in Track A:

Municipal price contagion (SAR): a demand shock in a coastal municipality propagates to neighbours through ρWy as buyers shift demand to adjacent markets with lower prices.

Tourism and amenity spillovers (SLX/SDM): a municipality’s tourism_beds_density in neighbouring municipalities affects its own prices through the WX channel — proximity to a tourist destination raises prices in adjacent municipalities even if those municipalities are not themselves tourist destinations.

Unobserved regional factors (SEM): coastal attractiveness, historical urban legacy, NUTS2 regional identity, and commuting sheds create correlated unobservables across geographically proximate municipalities, producing a spatially structured error process.

Interpreting the indicator correlation structure

With indicators fully populated across all 278 municipalities, the correlation matrix captures the true national cross-sectional association structure.

Urban collinearity — what the output shows: the urban-intensity cluster (population_density, share_artificialized, poi_density_total) is strongly intercorrelated: r(population_density, share_artificialized) = 0.88, r(population_density, poi_density_total) = 0.85. Including both in a single OLS would produce severe multicollinearity. share_education_higher is part of the same urban cluster conceptually, but its correlations are more moderate: r(education, population_density) = 0.59, r(education, share_artificialized) = 0.65, r(education, poi_density_total) = 0.61. Education is a distinct signal — not as tightly collinear with the purely physical-density indicators — and can be included alongside log_population_density in the OLS if variance inflation is monitored.

The secondary-residence duality: secondary_residence_share is strongly negatively correlated with employment_rate (r = −0.79) and moderately negatively correlated with population_density (r = −0.40) and share_education_higher (r = −0.52). This reflects the structural duality: secondary residence is high in two fundamentally different contexts — (1) Algarve coastal municipalities (very high prices, driven by international tourism investment and second-home demand) and (2) rural interior municipalities (very low prices, secondary homes owned by emigrants or retirees). These two groups pull the coefficient in opposite directions in OLS, depending on which other covariates are present.

Two distinct high-price mechanisms: the national data contains two structurally different pathways to high prices: 1. Metropolitan human-capital mechanism (Lisboa, Porto, Oeiras, Cascais): high density (3,741–5,597 persons/km²), high education share (28–36 %), high POI density, moderate secondary residence (~6–12 %). 2. Coastal tourism mechanism (Algarve municipalities — Lagos, Loulé, Aljezur, Vila do Bispo, Albufeira): low density (19–314 persons/km²), low education share (~12–15 %), very high secondary residence, high tourism beds. Education alone predicts this group poorly; secondary residence and tourism density are the key identifiers.

A single linear OLS does not cleanly separate these two mechanisms. Spatial models — particularly SEM and SDEM, which absorb spatially clustered unobservables such as the Algarve sub-market premium — can partly correct for this heterogeneity in the error structure.

The recommended parsimonious predictor set selects one representative from each family, conscious of the duality above: - Urban intensity: log_population_density - Human capital: share_education_higher (strongest bivariate predictor among those with known positive sign) - Age structure: share_age_65plus - Housing stock / tourist demand: secondary_residence_share (enters with ambiguous sign; see B7 for the actual OLS result) - Land use: share_artificialized - Vacancy / stock quality: vacant_total_share

Note: tourism_beds_density and poi_density_total were included in the initial recommended list but the collinearity screening may exclude one or both depending on the VIF threshold applied by valid_numeric_predictors. Check section B7’s output for the actual predictor set used in the model.

Interpreting the municipal maps

Price choropleth maps reveal two spatially distinct high-price clusters that confirm the structural duality identified in B3:

  1. Metropolitan corridor: Lisboa (€4,340/m²), Cascais (€4,053), Oeiras (€3,471), and Porto (€2,984) form dense, contiguous high-price clusters in the Great Lisbon and Porto Metropolitan Areas. The spatial gradient is steep: moving one or two municipalities inland from the core, prices drop by 30–50 %. This gradient motivates the SAR mechanism — price contagion spreading outward from the metropolitan core through demand-displacement cascades.

  2. Algarve coast: Lagos (€3,452), Loulé (€3,333), Aljezur (€3,059), Vila do Bispo (€3,030), Albufeira (€2,941), Tavira (€2,853) form a second contiguous cluster. This cluster is driven by international tourism investment and second-home demand, not local labour-market dynamics.

Interior municipalities (Alentejo, Beira Interior, Trás-os-Montes) form large contiguous low-price zones (€208–€500/m²). This creates a strong global spatial gradient that generates very high positive Moran’s I.

Missingness maps for sales: only 2 municipalities are missing — Barrancos (Alentejo, pop. ~1,700) and Penedono (Norte, pop. ~2,600). The estimation sample is functionally the entire mainland Portugal. These two absences create no material distortion in spatial weight matrices, Moran statistics, or regression coefficients.

Missingness maps for rent: 75 municipalities missing, geographically concentrated in interior regions. The missingness map will show a clearly non-random spatial pattern — a broad band of missing observations across the interior. This confirms structural market absence rather than random data loss. If the rent target is used in a sensitivity analysis (B10), the spatial weight matrix must be subsetted accordingly.

Indicator maps and their relationship to the price map: - population_density: high in Lisboa, Porto, Braga corridor — visually matches the metropolitan price cluster. - share_education_higher: mirrors population density but with lower spatial contrast — useful as a human-capital demand indicator. - secondary_residence_share: shows high values in TWO distinct spatial zones — Algarve coast and parts of Trás-os-Montes / Beira Interior — reflecting the duality discussed in B3. - tourism_beds_density: highly concentrated in Lisboa, Porto, and Algarve — visually matches the price map better than secondary residence for identifying high-price locations. - share_artificialized: follows the population density map, confirming high urban-rural correlation.

Interpreting the municipal weights diagnostics

All four weight matrices are built on the complete 278-municipality polygon layer. With 276/278 municipalities available for the primary regression sample, the spatial weight matrix used in estimation covers essentially the full national adjacency structure. This is qualitatively different from a subsampled W: subsampled matrices lose links at the boundary of the observed sample, potentially attenuating Moran statistics and spatial model parameters.

Size heterogeneity: Portugal’s municipal boundaries span an order-of-magnitude range in area. Alentejo municipalities (Mértola: 1,292 km²; Castro Verde: 568 km²) have typically 3–5 contiguous neighbours. Minho and Douro municipalities (Braga: 183 km², some as small as 8 km²) may have 8–12 neighbours. Queen contiguity preserves this natural heterogeneity, which is structurally informative — large rural municipalities with few neighbours form genuine spatial isolation in the price formation process. kNN-4 and kNN-6 impose a uniform neighbourhood size that helps statistical power in sparse regions but may artificially link geographically distant municipalities.

Islands (expected: zero): mainland Portugal’s municipal boundary topology is well-defined with no maritime municipalities entirely surrounded by other municipalities. Verify n_islands = 0 for Queen and Rook. Any non-zero island count indicates a geometry problem in the source GPKG (sliver polygons, topology gaps).

Queen vs. Rook similarity: for mainland Portugal, Queen and Rook contiguity produce nearly identical neighbour lists because most boundaries share extended edges rather than single corner points. Small cardinality differences at irregular boundary junctions (typically ±1 neighbour per municipality) should produce essentially the same Moran statistics and model conclusions.

Robustness criterion: the four W matrices serve as the robustness grid in B10. Spatial dependence findings that are consistent across Queen, Rook, kNN-4, and kNN-6 are robust to the adjacency definition and represent genuine spatial structure rather than a W-artefact.

Why high indicator Moran I motivates spatial modelling: even if OLS perfectly captures the indicator-price relationship in the non-spatial sense, the spatial structure within the indicators means that OLS residuals will absorb any spatial structure in prices not fully explained by the observed X variables — and that unexplained spatial structure generates autocorrelated residuals. Rent vs. sales Moran comparison (if available): the rent Moran I is estimated on 203 municipalities, deliberately excluding the lowest-price rural interior. Critical diagnostic finding — SEM and SDEM fail the residual Moran test:

Despite achieving the best AIC (SEM: −41.8) and second-best AIC (SDEM: −40.8), SEM residual Moran = 0.756 and SDEM residual Moran = 0.741 are substantially higher than the OLS residual Moran (0.620). SEM and SDEM did not absorb spatial autocorrelation — they worsened it. This is a fundamental diagnostic failure: the λ process in SEM/SDEM models the spatial correlation of the errors, but it does so in a way that — in this estimation — concentrates the spatial autocorrelation rather than distributing it. The reason is that λ_SEM = 0.848 and λ_SDEM = 0.837 are very large (close to the upper stability bound of 1), which can produce near-singular error covariance matrices and numerical instability.

SAR and SDM successfully absorb spatial autocorrelation: - SAR reduces residual Moran from 0.620 to 0.040 (94% reduction). - SDM reduces it to −0.009 (essentially zero; slight over-correction).

The AIC vs. residual Moran contradiction: the information criteria (AIC/LL) and the residual spatial diagnostics disagree on model quality for this dataset. AIC favours SEM and SDEM; residual Moran favours SAR and SDM. In spatial econometrics, both criteria are informative but serve different purposes. The residual Moran is a more direct test of whether the model has captured the spatial structure of the data — and by this criterion, SAR and SDM are clearly superior. Model selection should weight the residual Moran heavily when the goal is absorbing spatial dependence.

SAR ρ = 0.649: within the expected range for the national scale. The multiplier (1 − ρ)⁻¹ ≈ 2.85 implies that total impacts are roughly 2.85× the direct effects — substantial national cascade effects.

SDM ρ = 0.705, with WX term W_log_population_density: the SDM adds a spatial lag of log_population_density. The θ coefficient for W_log_population_density reflects whether municipalities surrounded by dense neighbours command higher or lower prices after controlling for own-municipality density. This is the key variable-specific spillover that SDM captures and SAR cannot.

Interpreting the spatial impact decomposition — Track B

Which models produce impacts, and why
Model Contains ρ (spatial lag on y)? Direct impact Indirect impact Total impact
OLS No β (raw coefficient) — —
SLX No β for own X; θ for WX 0 (local spillover only) β + θ (if WX included)
SAR Yes (I−ρW)⁻¹ β_k — average diagonal of S_k average off-diagonal of S_k direct + indirect
SDM Yes (I−ρW)⁻¹(β_k I + θ_k W) diagonal off-diagonal row sum direct + indirect
SEM No (λ on error) β (= error-corrected OLS β) 0 by construction = direct
SDEM No (λ on error, θ on WX) β / θ (local) 0 by construction = direct

Core rule: indirect effects on y exist only when the model contains an endogenous spatial lag ρWy. SEM and SDEM absorb spatial structure in the error term, not in y; no multiplier propagates shocks through the outcome network.

Reading the SAR impacts at municipal scale — what this run found

SAR ρ = 0.649. The spatial multiplier (I − ρW)⁻¹ produces economically large indirect effects. The indirect-to-direct ratio for all predictors equals ρ/(1−ρ) ≈ 1.85, so indirect effects are approximately 1.85× the direct effects for every predictor.

SEM and SDEM: explicit zero indirect effects on y

Why SEM indirect = 0: in y = Xβ + u, u = λWu + ε, a change in X_i affects y_i via β. It does not enter any other municipality’s structural equation. The λWu process captures spatially clustered unobservables — the Algarve sub-market premium, metropolitan agglomeration rents, coastal geography — that are autocorrelated in the error term. Correcting for this clustering changes β’s efficiency and standard errors but does not create propagation through the y network. However, as established in B8, SEM residual Moran (0.756) is larger than OLS (0.620) in this run, meaning the SEM λ process does not adequately represent the spatial dependence present in the data. SEM β coefficients are therefore not reliable here.

Why SDEM indirect = 0 on y: in y = Xβ + WXθ + u, u = λWu + ε, WXθ enters y as an observable regressor — a first-order local spillover from neighbours’ X values. A change in municipality j’s predictor raises municipality i’s price by θ × w_ij (a local effect proportional to the spatial weight). This does not propagate further through a multiplier chain. SDEM also fails the residual Moran test in this run (residual I = 0.741).

Which model is most defensible for Portugal? The residual Moran criterion clearly favours SDM (residual I ≈ 0): it absorbs the spatial autocorrelation that SEM and SDEM fail to address. The large ρ (0.705) in SDM confirms that demand-displacement cascades from metropolitan cores are a dominant mechanism in the Portuguese national housing market. The SEM mechanism (regional unobservables) may also be present but is not well-captured by the current estimation, possibly due to the extreme λ estimates near the stability boundary.

Policy interpretation of the total impact at national scale

With 276 municipalities in the system, the aggregate national impact of a localised policy intervention is substantial. For share_education_higher in SAR:

  • A 10-pp increase in higher-education share in one municipality (e.g., a university expansion in Évora):
    • Direct effect: SAR_direct × 0.10 = 5.609 × 0.10 ≈ 0.561 log-points ≈ 75% higher own-municipality price.
    • Indirect effect (distributed): SAR_indirect × 0.10 = 8.495 × 0.10 ≈ 0.850 log-points on average across all 275 other municipalities.
    • Total: SAR_total × 0.10 = 14.10 × 0.10 — the aggregate of direct and all indirect effects.

This is the central policy implication of spatial econometrics: ignoring spatial interdependence (the OLS baseline) underestimates the full national impact of localised housing-market interventions by the ratio SAR_total / OLS_beta ≈ 14.10 / 5.538 ≈ 2.55.

Interpreting the municipal robustness matrix

The robustness matrix systematically tests whether the spatial dependence finding and model conclusions survive changes in: (1) W specification (Queen, Rook, kNN-4, kNN-6); (2) the price outcome (sales total, sales existing, rent); and (3) whether the extreme top 1 % of price values is excluded (primarily Lisboa, Cascais, Oeiras — the extreme outliers of the national distribution).

Interpreting the results:

  • Residual Moran consistent across W specs: if all four W specifications produce statistically significant positive residual Moran after OLS, the spatial structure finding is robust to the adjacency definition. This is the expected result given the strong national price gradient.
  • Elhorst LM entry consistent across W specs: if the joint Robust LM_ρ / LM_λ pattern (i.e., which branch is entered) is the same across Queen, Rook, kNN-4, and kNN-6, the spatial dependence is a structural feature of the national housing market rather than an artefact of the adjacency definition. The LR simplification conclusions — SAR vs. SDM (LR_θ) and SEM vs. SDM (Common Factor LR_λ) — should also be stable across W specs to be credible.
  • Sales total vs. sales existing: both have near-complete coverage (276 and 274 municipalities). Model conclusions should be highly consistent. Minor coefficient differences are expected because the existing-sales market excludes new developments (concentrated in urban peripheries), which carry a structural price premium from newer building standards and amenities.
  • Rent as outcome (203 municipalities): if ρ (or λ) and coefficient signs are consistent with the sales model, the spatial mechanism generalises across tenure types. If they diverge — e.g., rent Moran lower, rent ρ smaller — this suggests that the rental market has different spatial integration, consistent with rent regulation, sticky rental prices, or sorting of lower-income households into rental housing predominantly in urban cores rather than spreading across the national system.
  • Excluding top 1 % of prices: removing the 2–3 most extreme municipalities tests whether the strong spatial dependence finding is driven by the Lisboa/Cascais/Oeiras outliers rather than the structural national pattern. If ρ drops substantially after exclusion, the spatial dependence is partly a leverage effect of extreme observations. If ρ remains large, the finding is genuinely distributed across the national housing system.

This multi-scale spatial dependence is theoretically expected: housing-market integration is a hierarchical process — within a city, zone-to-zone contagion follows neighbourhood comparison; across the country, municipal price diffusion follows demand displacement from metropolitan cores and regional demand shocks from national policy, tourism, and labour-market dynamics.

The two mechanisms differ: Track A spatial dependence reflects primarily the local transmission of price information and amenity diffusion within a geographically bounded urban system. 2.6 Limitations

Limitations of this analysis: - Asking prices are not transaction prices. Actual sales prices can differ substantially from listed prices, particularly in the Algarve where negotiating margins are large. - Listings are market-filtered: properties not listed on the observed platforms are absent. The dataset over-represents the active resale market and may underrepresent distressed, institutional, or publicly mediated transactions. - Aggregated municipal indicators hide within-municipality variation (the MAUP problem applies to Track B: the same point data produce different spatial patterns at different aggregation levels). - W is a modelling assumption. All results are conditional on the chosen W and require the robustness checks in B10 for credibility. - Significant spatial impacts are associations, not causal effects. No causal identification strategy is applied in this notebook. - The structural duality between the metropolitan human-capital mechanism and the Algarve tourism mechanism suggests that a single-regime linear spatial model is a simplification. A spatial regime model with separate ρ or β vectors for coastal/metropolitan vs. interior municipalities may be more appropriate for policy analysis. - beds_per_resident and share_tourism_beds_al (the latter with 7 missing values) were flagged in B2; the former is entirely empty and excluded from all models.
